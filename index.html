01 — Overview

What the Original Flo Got Wrong

Predictions were presented as single fixed dates without any indication of uncertainty. Educational content was gated behind tracking setup, language assumed menstruation, and articles lacked visible sources or review credentials. As a result, users who came to learn were structurally excluded, while tracking users were given overly certain information that could increase anxiety. These issues were not isolated interface problems, but structural outcomes of a design that prioritized one type of user and assumed all users would contribute personal data.

02 — Stakeholders

Who is this for?

VSD begins with a stakeholder analysis — identifying not just the primary users but the full range of people affected by the design. Two direct stakeholder groups in the redesign:

S1 — Cycle Trackers

Users who feel stress about not knowing where they are in their cycle

People who use the app primarily to understand where they are in their cycle, often managing anxiety about unpredictability. They are already tracking data and want that data to feel meaningful and honest, not alarming.

Wellbeing (intrinsic) Trustworthiness (instrumental)
S2 — Library Users

Users who come to learn about reproductive health

People who primarily want to learn about reproductive health, not to track a cycle. They need the app to be upfront about what is well-established, what is uncertain, and where the app may not be fully accurate or applicable to all users — rather than presenting all claims as equally authoritative facts.

Honesty (intrinsic) Factuality (instrumental)

03 — Problem Analysis

What values did the original Flo embed — and fail to embed?

Before designing anything, I mapped where the original app partially addressed stakeholder values and where it failed entirely. This audit became the foundation for specific, values-grounded design requirements.

Predictions shown as single fixed dates

Single fixed dates were presented as certain. A confident but incorrect prediction felt more harmful than uncertainty.

Educational content gated behind data entry

To read a health article, users must first complete a profile. This structurally refuses access to S2 — users who want to learn without sharing personal data.

Symptoms Without Context

Logging symptoms returned data without explanation. Users could not connect what they felt to their cycle phase, increasing confusion rather than reducing it.

04 — Value to Design Mapping

Translating values into visible interface features

One key learning from this process: design requirements only do their job if they are visible to the user. A requirement like "annual content audit" sounds meaningful but disappears into the backend. The real work is making values legible in the interface itself.

Wellbeing (S1)
→
Summary Mode on home screen (phase name + days to period + expected symptom); additional insights collapsed by default
Trustworthiness (S1)
→
Predictions as date ranges; confidence badges (High/Moderate/Low); Expandable panel explaining prediction logic and recent cycle history
Honesty (S2)
→
App discloses content limitations upfront — content type labels signal where claims are uncertain or may not apply to all readers before they begin
Factuality (S2)
→
"Reviewed by licensed OB-GYN" badge; "Last Reviewed" date; health claims presented as evidence-based ranges rather than single figures; "Sources Used" academic panel

05 — Design Decisions

Key changes in the redesign

1

Focus Mode — Home Screen

S1 · Wellbeing

Original problem

Home screen displayed multiple "My Daily Insights" widgets simultaneously — high information density that created cognitive overload rather than reassurance.

Redesign

  • Persistent "Designed for Focus" view: current phase, days to next period, expected symptom
  • Phase Connection: when a symptom is logged, a brief explanation + reassurance note appears immediately
  • All additional daily insights collapsed by default, expandable on tap
2

Prediction Details Screen

S1 · Trustworthiness

Original problem

Predictions showed as a single fixed date ("Prediction: Day of Ovulation") with no confidence information, no explanation, and no acknowledgement that cycles vary.

Redesign

  • All predictions display as date ranges (e.g., "Expected between April 2 and April 5")
  • Confidence badge (High / Moderate / Low) on every forecast
  • Panel explaining prediction logic
  • "Factors Affecting This Range" section lists recent logged events
  • Recent cycle lengths chart shows the data behind the prediction
3

Dual-Mode Onboarding

S2 · Honesty

Original problem

All educational content was gated behind a tracking setup flow. A user who wants to learn about reproductive health without tracking data had no path forward.

Redesign

  • Onboarding presents two equal-weight paths: "Track My Cycle" and "Explore Health Library"
  • Library mode gives full access to all articles with zero data entry required
  • Settings allow switching between modes at any time
4

Health Library Article Pages

S2 · Factuality + Honesty

Original problem

Articles presented health claims as facts with no sourcing, no medical review credential, no date, and no acknowledgement of evidence quality.

Redesign

  • Content type label before the reader begins: High Evidence / Emerging Evidence / Lived Experience
  • "Reviewed by a licensed OB-GYN" badge on applicable articles
  • "Last Reviewed" date displayed near article title
  • Our commitment badge
  • "Sources Used" academic panel at article footer
  • Articles explicitly state when knowledge is incomplete or not universally applicable, rather than implying certainty
5

Privacy Status Card

S1 + S2 · Trust

Original problem

No visible summary of what data Flo stores, where it is stored, or how to delete it. Trust in health apps depends on data transparency.

Redesign

  • Privacy Status card on home screen lists stored data (locally on device vs shared)
  • Direct 2-click "Delete My Data" action — no buried settings menu required

06 — Value Tensions

Where values conflict

The core tension in this redesign was between S1 trustworthiness and S2 honesty. More specifically, it was a tension between data-driven accuracy and transparent acknowledgement that the app’s knowledge and predictions may be incomplete or not fully accurate: S1 needs the app to feel reliable and confident to reduce cycle anxiety, while S2 needs the app to openly communicate what it does not know and where content has limitations.

S1 value

Trustworthiness via tracking data

S1 needs predictions and health content to feel dependable. Surfacing too much uncertainty can increase anxiety rather than reduce it, working against the wellbeing goal.

vs

S2 value

Honesty about content limitations

S2 needs the app to be transparent about what is clinically established versus emerging or uncertain, and to acknowledge where health information may not apply to all bodies or situations — without softening or hiding those caveats.

Resolution: This tension is addressed by separating how uncertainty is communicated across different features. Prediction-related interfaces (e.g., cycle forecasts) prioritize trustworthiness by limiting the visibility of uncertainty to maintain a sense of reliability for S1. In contrast, health content explicitly foregrounds honesty through the content type label system, where levels of evidence and limitations are clearly signaled. By separating prediction features from informational content, the design ensures that S1’s need for dependable guidance is not undermined by excessive caveats, while still allowing S2’s need for transparency and factual clarity to be fully supported. This separation ensures that honesty does not undermine trustworthiness, but is instead expressed in contexts where users are actively seeking explanation rather than reassurance.

07 — Feedback & Iteration

How feedback shaped the final design

Assignment 4 required showing the prototype to real users and gathering specific feedback. I conducted informal walkthroughs with cycle-tracking app users (S1) and peers who use health apps primarily for learning (S2).

S1 Walkthrough — Prediction Screen

Asked 2–3 cycle-tracking app users to walk through the Prediction Details screen. Key finding: the confidence badge labeling was initially unclear. Revised: added a one-line plain-language explanation below each badge.

S2 Walkthrough — Library Mode Onboarding

Asked peers who use health apps for reading whether the Library entry path felt genuinely welcoming and equal in weight to the tracking path. Key finding: the visual hierarchy initially de-emphasized the Library path. Revised: gave both paths identical button sizes and vertical positioning, with explicit label parity.

Peer Review (Assignment 3 → 4)

Peer reviewers flagged that the original design had too many visible badges on article pages, creating visual noise rather than trust signals. Revised: grouped the credentialing elements into a collapsible section at the top, with a single visible summary line. This made the information available without front-loading every article with four badges.
